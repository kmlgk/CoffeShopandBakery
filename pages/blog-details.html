The Home Pour-Over, Dialled In

June 24, 2025  ·  Nora Adeyemi  ·  6 min read

There's a quiet ritual to pour-over coffee that no machine can quite replicate. Just you, some good beans, and a few minutes of gentle attention. The good news? Café-quality filter at home isn't about expensive kit — it's about a handful of variables you can actually control.

Start with Fresh, Good Beans

Everything downstream depends on this. Buy whole beans, roasted within the last few weeks, and grind them just before you brew. A medium grind — think coarse sea salt — is your starting point for pour-over. Too fine and it turns bitter; too coarse and it runs thin and sour.

"Coffee is 98% water. If your water tastes off, so will your cup — filtered is your friend."

The Recipe We Use at the Bar

We keep a ratio of roughly 1 gram of coffee to 16 grams of water. For a single mug, that's about this:

Coffee22 g, medium grind
Water350 g, just off the boil (94°C)
Bloom50 g water, wait 40 seconds
Total brew time2:45 – 3:15

1. The Bloom

Pour just enough water to wet all the grounds — about twice the weight of your coffee — and wait. You'll see it puff up and bubble as trapped CO₂ escapes. This "bloom" lets the coffee brew evenly. Rushing it is the most common home mistake.

2. Pour in Slow Circles

Add the rest of the water in two or three slow, spiralling pours, keeping the bed level. Aim for a gentle, steady stream from the centre outward — you're rinsing flavour out, not blasting it.

3. Taste & Adjust

  • Too sour or weak? Grind a little finer, or slow your pour.
  • Too bitter or harsh? Grind a little coarser, or cool your water slightly.
  • Just right? Write it down. Consistency is the whole game.

Dial it in over a few mornings and you'll land on a cup that's clean, sweet, and unmistakably yours. And if you'd rather someone else fuss over the kettle — well, that's what we're here for.
